Marriage and Family

Women in KCP are vastly, vastly more likely than men or individuals of unknown gender to appear in litigation with a heterosexual partner or a family member. Even discounting miscellaneous family status, a majority - 56% - of women appearing in KCP appear with heterosexual partners. 70% of women appear with a partner or family member. So only 30% of women in KCP - less than a third!! - appear independent of a relative. In contrast, 70% of men and 100% of individuals of unknown gender appear independent of a relative.

Operating Together: Man “and his wife”

Most of the couples appear on the same side of the litigation, operating essentially as a unit.

Unsurprisingly, when one spouse is a more prominent character than the other in the fact pattern, the more prominent character is a man.

Two examples here

See Jannush v. Naffziger, KCP 149 (“Gene Jannusch and his wife, Martha, brought this action…” Gene appears prominently in the rest of the background section, but Martha never reappears again: “Gene acknowledged,” “Gene suggested,” “Gene attended the first of two festivals”); Dodson v. Shrader, KCP 572 (The plaintiff “purchased a used 1984 pick-up truck from Burns and Mary Shrader.” The Shraders are referred to collectively several times, but Mr. Shrader is the only one to act independently: “Mr. Shrader, through counsel, declined;” “At the time of the circuit court trial, according to [Mr.] Shrader”)


My favorite example of this phenomenon is in Ray v. William Eurice & Bros.:

Mrs. Ray couldn’t get a babysitter

Ray v. William G. Eurice & Bros., Inc 201 Md. 115 (1952), KCP 37

With the same grammatical flair as the judge in Jannusch, the judge here introduces the Rays as “Calvin T. Ray and Katherine S. J. Ray, his wife.” While Calvin Ray is a very prominent character (“Mr. Ray then employed an architect;” “Mr. Ray had copies;” “Mr. Ray advised Mr. Eurice.”)

The only time that Mrs. Ray acts independently is this: > On February 22, at the office of the Eurice Corporation, on the Old Philadelphia Road, the contract was signed. Present, at the time, were Mr. Ray - Mrs. Ray was absent and had signed the contract earlier because she could not a babysitter…


The Adversaries

Three KCP cases tackle difficulties of heterosexual relationships. Watts v. Watts and Chen v. Chen are about the unique conflicts resulting from separation, while the surrogacy case is about the conflict resulting from a deviation from the norm of one man and one woman having “biological children.”

Relevant Non-Party Spouses

Two men and two women had their relationships described with reference to the proceedings but without their partner appearing.

Sondra Locke and Clint

Locke v. Warner Bros., Inc.

57 Cal. App. 4th 354 (1997); KCP 513

Sondra Locke’s dispute with Warner Bros. is framed as something patronizingly set up for her by her ex Clint Eastwood (not a party), relevant to the argument about good faith.


Coretta Scott King

King v. Trustees of Boston Univ. 420 Mass. 52 (1995); KCP 237

Coretta Scott King brings her case on behalf of her family estate, and is actually a great example of rhetoric relating a woman to a husband but still giving her full agency: > “The plaintiff, Coretta Scott King, in her capacity as administratix of the estate of her late husband, and in her individual capacity, appeals…”

The issues with that case are far more salient with respect to race than gender.


The brother of Katz’s wife

Katz v. Danny Dare Inc. 610 S.W.2d 121 (Mo. Ct. App. 1980); KCP 248

I.G. Katz brought a promissory estoppel claim against a company the president of which “was also the brother of Katz’s wife,” establishing the connection between Katz and the company; moreover, his wife would benefit from a Social Security clause in the promised contract.


Pelo’s wife

Credit Bureau Enter. v. Pelo

608 N.W.2d 20 (Iowa 2000); KCP 297

Russell N. Pelo was involuntarily committed to a hospital after he telephoned his wife to make threats of self harm, relevant to the context of his refusal to pay.

I don’t give it the attention it deserves here, but there’s definitely an interesting conversation to be had with this case about ability, capacity, mental wellness, and bodily autonomy.


Barely Relevant Non-Party Spouses

One man and one woman had their marital status described as a strange, but seemingly purposeful, aside.

Dohrmann was a 40-year-old neurosurgeon, married to Dr. Helen Dohrmann.

Dohrmann v. Swaney

20 Cal. Rptr. 3d 507 (Cal. Ct. App. 2011); KCP 255

This is one of the cases about a vulnerable widow being taken advantage of. The court affirmed a summary judgment that a contract between Virginia H. Rogers, offering over $5.5 million in assets upon her death, and George Dohrmann, promising to add Rogers as a middle name for both of his sons, was unenforceable because the consideration was illusory (and “so grossly adequate as to shock the conscience.”)

Dohrmann is first introduced merely as “the appellant” and Rogers as “deceased (The Estate). The next information revealed is that Dohrmann was Rogers’ neighbor, and had minor children. The first robust introduction that Judge Fitzgerald Smith of the Illinois Appellate Court gave to the characters is as follows, in the”Background" section:

At the time they met, Mrs. Rogers was 73-year-old widow. She had never had nor adopted any children. Dohrmann was a 40-year-old neurosurgeon, married to Dr. Helen Dohrmann.

As I explore more thoroughly in the widows section, Mrs. Rogers’ old age and husbandlessness play into a sense of vulnerability, which explicitly ties into the court’s ruling of illusory consideration and dictum that the case could have been found void on the grounds of “unfairness” alone. The judge repeatedly mentions that Mrs. Rogers was not only old, but also a widow, harnessing general cultural assumptions about vulnerability to bolster their argument.

What value does the inclusion of Dohrmann’s job, his marriage, and his wife’s job add to the opinion? Perhaps Dohrmann’s marital status establishes that he was not as sad and lonely as Rogers is implied to be, or merely to contextualize his having children. The more interesting tidbit is the inclusion of both of their jobs. Simply in terms of gender representation, a side mention as “Dr. Helen Dohrmann” implies status, power, and wealth not usually associated with women. But it’s unlikely that Judge Fitzgerald Smith’s goal was to subtly challenge gender stereotypes. Perhaps the occupations establish that the Dohrmanns were well off, ruling out any moral defense based on need.


Claudia Aceves, a married woman

Aceves v. U.S. Bank N.A.

20 Cal. Rptr. 3d 507 (Cal. Ct. App. 2011); KCP 255

The judge introduces the plaintiff, independently bringing a suit with whose spouse appears only as a minor detail in the rest of the opinion, as “Claudia Aceves, a married woman.” (255) The case is about bankruptcy – perhaps her marriage establishes some kind of moral credibility? There might be some kind of difference in the judge’s mind between a bankrupted single woman and a bankrupted wife.
