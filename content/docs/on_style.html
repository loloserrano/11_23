“Frugging KCP” and the word “Fruggish” are intentionally irreverent, and I think Frug wouldn’t mind.

The following is from a section titled “On Style” in Frug’s last work, “A Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto (An Unfinished Draft).”

The medium is the message

Style is important in postmodern work. The medium is the message, in some cases … when style is salient, it is characterized by irony and by wordplay that is often dazzlingly funny, smart, and irreverent. Things aren’t just what they seem.

Mary Joe Frug, A Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto (An Unfinished Draft), Harvard Law Review 105 No. 5 1048 (Mar. 1992).

Adjectivization

In a project that I co-authored (pending publication, so I’ll be light on details) we reviewed a large sample of academic book reviews. Over 90 mens’ names were somehow “adjectivized” within the reviews - Foucauldian, Wittgensteinian, Hegelian - or “nouned” - Marxism, Darwinism - as opposed to only three women. This coincided with a qualitative pattern of men writing very favorably about other men, to fairly adulatory extremes.

The outcome we proposed in that paper is not that more women get sycophantic praise in book reviews, but to tone the personal veneration down in general. It can be dangerous to elevate scholars to lofty, elite status.

It Sounds Funny

So the choice of Frugging and Fruggish adopts this largely masculine technique - a sort of grammatical veneration, recognizing the greatness of (usually) a man and his ideas. I think Frug and Frug’s ideas deserve acknowledgment, study, replication, respect, and even admiration, but part of a liberation-minded postmodernist study is questioning power and stopping short of veneration.

Irony

Irony, for example, is a stylistic method of acknowledging and challenging a dominant meaning, of saying something and simultaneously denying it.

Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto 1047.

So I am choosing to adjectivize and verbify Frug. I acknowledge the dominant meaning of adjectivization, which is heavily masculine and uncomfortably adulatory. So I do it, but challenge it at the same time: apply it to a woman, and make a bit of fun. I think that these grammatical techniques, notwithstanding their weird connotations, are cool and interesting ways of honoring legacies. This project is directly inspired by Frug’s work, so she ought to occupy a central position. I verbify with reservations, and I express my reservations through silliness. Frugging says and denies itself.
