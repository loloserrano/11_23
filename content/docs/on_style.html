On Style

“Frugging KCP” is intentionally irreverent, but I think Frug wouldn’t mind.

The following is from a section titled “On Style” in Frug’s last work, “A Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto (An Unfinished Draft).”

The medium is the message

Style is important in postmodern work. The medium is the message, in some cases … when style is salient, it is characterized by irony and by wordplay that is often dazzlingly funny, smart, and irreverent. Things aren’t just what they seem.

Mary Joe Frug, A Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto (An Unfinished Draft), Harvard Law Review 105 No. 5 1048 (Mar. 1992).

Adjectivization

In a project that I co-authored (pending publication, so I’ll be light on details) we reviewed a large sample of academic book reviews. Over 90 mens’ names were somehow “adjectivized” within the reviews - Foucauldian, Wittgensteinian, Hegelian - or “nouned” - Marxism, Darwinism - as opposed to only three women. This coincided with a qualitative pattern of men writing very favorably about other men, to adulatory extremes in few (but very notable) cases.

The outcome we proposed in that paper was not to give more women sycophantic praise in book reviews, but to tone the personal veneration down in general. It can be dangerous to elevate scholars to lofty, elite status. Idolizing a certain person’s (usually a certain man’s) ideas can lead to the exclusion of new, challenging ideas, whether they be Jesus, Michel Foucault, or Tucker Carlson. Ideas and people should be challenged and reconsidered on a regular basis.

It Sounds Funny

So the choice of Frugging and Fruggish adopts this largely masculine technique - a sort of grammatical veneration, recognizing the greatness of (usually) a man and his ideas. I think Frug and Frug’s ideas deserve acknowledgment, study, replication, respect, and even admiration, but part of a liberation-minded postmodernist study is questioning power and stopping short of veneration.

Irony

Irony, for example, is a stylistic method of acknowledging and challenging a dominant meaning, of saying something and simultaneously denying it.

Postmodern Feminist Legal Manifesto 1047.

So I am choosing to adjectivize and verbify Frug. I acknowledge the dominant meaning of adjectivization, which is heavily masculine and uncomfortably adulatory. So I do it, but challenge it at the same time: apply it to a woman, and make a bit of fun. I think that these grammatical techniques, notwithstanding their problematic connotations, are cool and interesting ways of honoring legacies. This project is directly inspired by Frug’s work, so she ought to occupy a central position. I verbify and adjectivize with reservations, and I express my reservations through silliness. “Fruggish” says and denies itself.

A Reservation

I would be remiss to not address the immense injustice that has impacted Frug’s legacy. Frug is also known for having been viciously mocked in a close-minded, unprofessional, and frankly cruel article distributed at a Harvard Law Review banquet on the anniversary of her death. Craig Coben and Ken Fenyo’s sick attempts at humor are not the kind of postmodern strategy she wrote about, nor the type of balancing act I’m trying to make here. I have immense respect for Frug, and I have reason to believe that that my choice reflects that.
